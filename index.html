How to Use Journalism To Stay Connected To Your Local Community

Time to Complete: If you do it right, your work will never truly be done
Awesome rating: Pretty cool

In our fast-moving modern world, globalized news takes up the lion’s share of people’s attention. National and international headlines are the main focus. This increase of globalized media has brought the world closer together, but at the cost of people being familiar with their local communities. In this guide, I outline a step-by-step way to know your local community like the back of your hand, using nothing but journalism.

  • Things You'll Need

  • A curious mind
  • The ability to read
  • An electronic device (smartphone, computer, etc.)
  • Internet access
  • Print media (newspapers, magazines, etc.)
  • A radio (AM/FM, like the one in your car)

  1. Know your local publications/sources.

    Everything: local newspapers (print and online), local news channels, local magazines, local radio (music, news, or talk), all of it. If they share news-—or really any sort of journalism—-within your local community, make sure you know they exist.

  2. Read/watch/listen/etc.

    Turn on the TV and watch the local news channel (first thing in the morning, at night, whenever), buy the local newspaper for a couple bucks, sit down, and read the whole thing, put on a local radio station in your car instead of Spotify or CD’s and listen to a DJ’s show or listen to the news, subscribe to the local paper online (it’s usually only a dollar or so per month; you can spare it) and read the articles and newsletters, or pick up free print publications in restaurants and grocery stores. Absorb as much as you can. In today’s age, just doing one of these things regularly will connect you locally better than half the population. Become well-read on the things happening in and affecting your town.

  3. Engage with what you’re learning about

    Check out the stuff you read or hear about. Watch your local sports (minor leagues, college, high school, and pro teams if you’re in a major city). Go to the summer farmers markets and food truck nights downtown. Go out for eats at the new local restaurant that’s getting plenty of buzz. Go see live music and local bands that get a little shoutout or interview with an indie publication or that you hear about on local radio. Explore your city with local news as a guide.

  4. Be a part of the community

    Go to plenty of these events. Get outside, walk around your town, and be an active member of the community. See everything your town has to offer. Local journalism can tell you everything you need to know.

  5. Make your own opinions on issues

    After becoming well-read on your town’s dealings, dive deeper. Go beyond surface-level knowledge and comprehension and spend time thinking about it. Come to your own conclusions, guided by the facts learned from local journalism. Back yourself up, too.

  6. Discuss local issues with other community members

    Talk about these things with other people around town. Hear their sides and thoughts and present your own. Make sure you know what you’re talking about, and that you’re not just spouting nonsense. Keep talks respectful and go in with an open mind. Discussions like this, about local issues with other local people, are an excellent way to connect yourself with the community.

  7. Get involved!

    After going to plenty of community events and discussing community issues, you should be pretty well-connected by now. The next step is to get involved. Donate to local causes or start your own. Volunteer. Root for your local teams and regularly dine at your local restaurants. Put on events for the community and book local bands. Raise money for local issues. Help people search for missing dogs, cats, or children. Take everything you’ve learned about your community and your town from local news and journalism and apply it!

  8. (Optional) Try your own hand at local journalism

    By now, you should know your community pretty well. Why not help others know it as good as you? Try writing a column for the local paper, starting a local magazine, or getting a timeslot on local radio. Why not? Have some fun with it.
